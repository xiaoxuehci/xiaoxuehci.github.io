What If Workplace AI Refused to Help the Boss?

As generative AI enters knowledge work, workers are not only users of AI tools, but also people whose labor, expertise, and routines are being reorganized through them. Tools that promise to help employees write faster, summarize better, or coordinate work more smoothly may also make their knowledge easier to extract, their performance easier to compare, and their everyday activities more available to managerial interpretation.

This project asks what it would mean to imagine workplace AI from the worker's side. I am running a speculative co-design workshop around a deliberately provocative prompt: an AI tool that helps workers, but refuses to help bosses. The prompt is not a literal product proposal. Instead, it is a way to make visible the hidden politics of workplace AI: who benefits from automation, what kinds of worker knowledge should not be captured, and when "support" becomes acceleration, surveillance, or control.

Methodologically, the project draws on the logic of Magic Machine workshops in design research, using imaginative making to help participants move beyond feasible product thinking. Rather than asking workers to evaluate existing AI tools, the workshop invites them to create impossible, partial, or contradictory AI concepts that express their concerns, desires, boundaries, and forms of refusal.

Through this process, the project explores how knowledge workers understand support, risk, and agency under organizational AI deployment. More broadly, it asks what technology design can and cannot do when AI is shaped by workplace productivity demands, managerial power, and capitalist incentives.
