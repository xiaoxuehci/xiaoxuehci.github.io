How Non-AI Experts Enact Design-in-Use with Commercial LLMs in the Wild

Commercial LLMs are often discussed as tools to be prompted, personalized, or used more effectively. In our study, we examine a different phenomenon: how non-AI experts enact design-in-use with commercial LLMs in everyday life.

Working with twelve participants, we used a participatory method we call co-archiving to understand long-term shaping practices through participants' own cases, traces, and reflections. These cases included AI-supported reading for children, writing and study workflows, emotionally responsive companions, and models configured around personal values, standards, and situations.

Our findings show that people do not only design prompts. They work on multiple objects of design-in-use: model outputs, reusable prompts, workflows, agents, contexts, memories, and sometimes the perceived character of the model itself. Through these practices, users try to make commercial LLMs answerable to what matters in their lives, from local adequacy and practical support to care, recognition, relation, and imaginative possibility.

Yet this shaping often happens without system visibility. Participants could rarely know exactly what the model remembered, why it changed, or whether their efforts would persist. They inferred influence through unstable signs such as tone, consistency, refusal behavior, and task performance.

This makes user agency both meaningful and precarious. Commercial LLMs open new spaces for non-AI experts to participate in AI design through use, but platforms still govern the infrastructures that make such design possible: memory, model updates, safety policies, interface options, and persistence.

By foregrounding meaning-making, agency, and contestability, this project asks what it means to take everyday LLM tailoring seriously as design. It connects to discussions in design-in-use, end-user development, personalization, participatory AI, AI companions, platform governance, and accountability. More broadly, it asks how HCI should respond when people are already designing AI systems through use, but without full access to the systems that shape what their design work can become.
