Uneven Participation in AI-mediated Group Learning

LLMs are often imagined as personal tutors or productivity tools for individual learners. But when AI enters a group setting, it does more than help students produce answers. It can quietly reshape who gets to participate, whose ideas become visible, and who remains at the edge of the process.

This work-in-progress project examines those participation asymmetries in a winter camp with high school students from Beijing and Tibet. The groups brought together students from different educational and technological contexts. Some students had frequent experience with AI tools, presentation software, and laptop-based project work, while others were more familiar with mobile-based search or had less experience using AI for open-ended school tasks. This made the camp a valuable setting for studying how collaboration around AI is negotiated in practice.

We designed a short AI-mediated group task followed by a co-design activity. This allowed us to observe how students actually used AI in a time-bounded group setting, then invite them to identify moments where AI helped, excluded, redirected, or made participation harder to negotiate.

What emerged was not simply a story of AI making students more efficient. AI became a group resource, but not one that everyone could access or contest equally. Device control, prompting skill, confidence, and prior AI experience shaped who could speak through the AI and whose ideas reached the final output. In some groups, one student became the main interface between the group and the model, while students with less AI experience or less confidence in the task stayed at the edge of the process, even when they had ideas to contribute.

These moments matter because they are easy to overlook. Nothing necessarily breaks: the group still produces an answer, the AI still appears useful, and the activity still moves forward. Yet the process can depend on subtle exclusions, delegated authority, and forms of dependence that become harder to notice once AI is treated as a neutral helper.

The co-design activity asks students to move from situated experience to possible intervention. Rather than only asking what AI should generate, we ask what forms of visibility, turn-taking, explanation, and collective control might help groups notice and respond to uneven participation before it becomes embedded in the learning process.

My contribution focused on the methodological design of the co-design activity, especially how to move from students' situated AI use to reflection on future AI for collaborative learning. I also conducted reflexive thematic analysis with my co-authors, and we are currently writing this study as a research paper.
