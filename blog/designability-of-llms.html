[Work in Progress] Designability of LLMs: When Everyday Use Becomes Design

Large language models are no longer just tools given by companies or experts—they are constantly being reshaped by the people who use them. Yet most discussions still frame this only as “prompt engineering” or “personalization.”

In our recent study, we worked with twelve non-expert participants to understand how they actually tailor LLMs in everyday life. Using a participatory method we call co-archiving, participants collected and shared their own cases—from turning GPT into a step-by-step math tutor, to building a genderless companion that felt “truly human,” to creating a child’s reading partner.

Taking Design-in-Use as a theorical lens, what we found is that tailoring is rarely planned as a traditional design process does. It unfolds as unplanned, improvised, and ongoing processes, blurring the line between using and designing. People brought different orientations—some treating the model as a precise tool, others cultivating it as a partner. Along the way, they encountered tensions: limited memory control, opaque interfaces, disruptive platform updates, and fragile senses of ownership.

To capture these practices, we introduced the concept of designability—the extent to which people can iteratively reshape a model through use, aligning it with their needs, values, and ways of living. Designability highlights tailoring as a site of bottom-up empowerment, while also pointing to the risks when platform power overshadows people’s efforts.

This project invites us to rethink an ethical question: if people are already designing in use, what are the new roles of designers who usually legalize works with interventionism in HAI ?
